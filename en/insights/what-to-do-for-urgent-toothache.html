What Should You Do for an Urgent Toothache?

Severe toothache, night-time throbbing, facial swelling, or sudden sensitivity can quickly affect sleep and daily comfort. In these situations, the goal is not only temporary relief but also getting the problem evaluated properly without delay.

4 min read Archident Insights

What can be done at home first?

When the pain starts, avoid putting extra pressure on the area, stay away from very hot or very cold foods, and keep oral hygiene gentle and consistent. If food is trapped between teeth, careful cleaning may provide some relief.

If there is swelling, a short cold application from the outside of the face may help. Placing aspirin directly on the tooth or using strong heat is not recommended.

Which symptoms require urgent evaluation?

Swelling, throbbing pain, fever, a bad taste in the mouth, increasing sensitivity while chewing, fracture after trauma, or bleeding should all prompt contact with a dentist without delay.

Pain that wakes you up at night, lasts longer than two days, or does not settle despite pain relief should not only be monitored at home.

FAQ

Quick questions about urgent toothache guidance

Should I start antibiotics immediately for urgent tooth pain?

No. Whether antibiotics are needed should be decided by a dentist based on the examination and clinical findings. Not every toothache requires antibiotics.

If there is swelling, should I use heat or cold?

A short cold application from the outside of the face is generally preferred. Heat may worsen symptoms in some situations.
